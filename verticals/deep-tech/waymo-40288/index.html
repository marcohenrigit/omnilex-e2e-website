We know your business

You are insuring a driver that is software. This is the legal layer under that risk.

Standing up the insurance and risk infrastructure for a fully autonomous fleet carries a very specific legal footprint, one that no off-the-shelf motor policy was built for. From Waymo’s public model and how an insurance-infrastructure team operates, here is where we would start, and all of it recurs in every new market you enter.

01

Liability when the driver is software

Allocating fault and coverage when there is no human at the wheel. Passenger, third-party and product-liability lines have to be mapped to Waymo’s actual operating model, not to a policy written for a human driver.

02

State and city financial-responsibility filings

Every jurisdiction sets its own AV insurance minimums, self-insurance and financial-responsibility rules. Each new market means filings, certificates and proof of coverage kept current as you expand.

03

Claims-handling frameworks

When an incident happens, the process has to be defined in advance: notice, evidence including sensor and telematics data, subrogation and settlement authority, so a claim resolves in days rather than turning into litigation.

04

Rider terms and insurance disclosures

The rider agreement, waivers and insurance disclosures every passenger accepts before a ride, drafted so coverage, liability and data use are clear and enforceable across markets.

05

Fleet, depot and partner insurance

Contracts with fleet operators, depots, maintenance and vehicle partners each carry insurance, indemnity and risk-allocation terms that have to line up with your master program rather than contradict it.

06

Captive, reinsurance and program structure

As you self-insure a novel risk, the captive, reinsurance and program-wording documents behind it have to be built and kept consistent as loss data accumulates and regulation moves.
